When proving the boundary layer equations, variables such as the streamwise distance, velocities, and the vertical coordinate are converted to nondimensional forms by dividing them by characteristic scales. For example, x which measures distance along the flow direction, is divided by a characteristic length such as the length of the plate or body under consideration. This results in a nondimensional horizontal coordinate that ranges from zero to one or beyond, depending on how far along the plate you measure. Similarly, the velocity component along this streamwise direction is made nondimensional by dividing by the free stream velocity, which is the speed of the fluid far upstream of the surface

The vertical coordinate, which measures the distance perpendicular to the surface, is treated differently. Because the boundary layer is usually very thin compared to the characteristic length, it is more meaningful to scale the vertical variable by the boundary layer thickness at that position along the plate rather than by the total plate length. This thickness itself grows with downstream distance and depends on Reynolds number and flow conditions. This choice of scaling for the vertical coordinate highlights the thinness of the velocity profile region next to the wall where viscous effects are significant.

Some more insigths into the X momentum Boundary Layer Equation (AI)

Because the boundary layer thickness is very thin compared to the length of the body, velocity gradients perpendicular to the surface (in the y direction) are much larger than those along the flow (in the x direction). As a result, viscous diffusion terms involving second derivatives with respect to y dominate over those with respect to x. This allows neglecting the viscous term involving the second derivative with respect to x. Furthermore, since the flow is steady and laminar in many cases, time derivatives are also omitted.

The y momentum equation in the boundary layer approximation is quite different from the usual full Navier-Stokes y momentum equation. In the boundary layer, the vertical or normal velocity component and variations happen over a very thin distance compared to the streamwise direction. Due to this, the vertical acceleration terms and viscous terms in the y direction become very small and can often be neglected. One key outcome is that the pressure gradient in the y direction within the boundary layer is negligible, meaning the pressure does not change through the thickness of the boundary layer in the y direction. Instead, the pressure within the boundary layer is taken as the same as the pressure in the external inviscid flow, which depends only on the x coordinate.

Some Insights

  • At low Mach numbers, the assumption that the pressure gradient in the vertical (normal) direction is negligible holds true because compressibility effects are small. This means the pressure is essentially constant across the boundary layer thickness in the y direction, while pressure changes occur primarily along the flow direction (x direction). Therefore, the classic boundary layer equation includes a significant pressure gradient in x but assumes none in y.
  • However, at high Mach numbers, especially in hypersonic regimes, compressibility effects become very important. Your point about the inverse Mach number times gamma being of the order of boundary layer thickness reflects the mathematical scaling used in hypersonic boundary layer theory. When Mach number is very large, the vertical pressure gradient cannot be ignored. Instead, it becomes a finite and important quantity that influences flow characteristics such as shock-boundary layer interactions and thermal effects.
  • In this high Mach number limit, the x momentum equation's pressure gradient term in the flow direction becomes negligible in comparison, meaning that pressure does not vary significantly along the surface within the boundary layer. Conversely, the pressure gradient in the y direction stays relevant and must be accounted for. This reversal of which pressure gradients matter compared to the low Mach number case is fundamental to hypersonic boundary layer analysis and leads to different flow behaviors and solution techniques.
